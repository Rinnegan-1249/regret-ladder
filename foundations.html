THE GAME MODEL

Foundations: Extensive-Form Games

The formal model everything on this site runs on — sequential decision-making with imperfect information — following Osborne & Rubinstein's definition as used by Zinkevich et al. (2007) [1] and Lanctot et al. (2009) [2].

01 /Extensive games with imperfect information

A finite extensive game with imperfect information consists of:

  • A finite set \(N\) of players, and a finite set \(H\) of histories (sequences of actions, closed under prefixes). \(Z \subseteq H\) are the terminal histories; \(A(h) = \{a : ha \in H\}\) are the actions available after non-terminal \(h\).
  • A player function \(P(h) \in N \cup \{c\}\) naming who acts after \(h\) — possibly chance \(c\), which acts with fixed known probabilities \(f_c(a|h)\) (the card deals in poker).
  • For each player, a partition of their decision histories into information sets \(I \in \mathcal{I}_i\): histories the player cannot distinguish, at which they must therefore act with one distribution. In Kuhn Poker, "I hold the Queen and the opponent bet" is one information set containing two histories — one where the opponent holds the Jack, one the King.
  • Utilities \(u_i(z)\) at terminal histories. If \(N = \{1, 2\}\) and \(u_1 = -u_2\) the game is zero-sum; \(\Delta_{u,i}\) denotes the utility range.
Perfect recall. All algorithms here assume players never forget their own past observations and actions: any two histories in the same information set share the player's own action-observation sequence. This is what makes the prefix \(z[I]\) of a terminal history unique and the counterfactual decomposition of the Regret section sound — and it is an explicit precondition of the MCCFR theorems.

02 /Strategies and reach probabilities

A behavioral strategy \(\sigma_i\) assigns a distribution over \(A(I)\) to every information set of player \(i\); a strategy profile \(\sigma\) collects one per player, and \(\sigma_{-i}\) is everyone but \(i\). The reach probability of a history factors by player:

$$\pi^{\sigma}(h) \;=\; \prod_{i \in N \cup \{c\}} \pi^{\sigma}_i(h) \;=\; \pi^{\sigma}_i(h)\,\pi^{\sigma}_{-i}(h),$$

where \(\pi^{\sigma}_i(h)\) is the product of player \(i\)'s own action probabilities along \(h\), and \(\pi^{\sigma}_{-i}(h)\) collects everyone else's including chance. \(\pi^{\sigma}(h, z)\) denotes the probability of completing \(h\) into \(z\). This factorization is the workhorse of the whole site: counterfactual values weight by \(\pi^{\sigma}_{-i}\) (what the world does), average strategies weight by \(\pi^{\sigma}_i\) (what the player does), and external-sampling MCCFR works precisely because sampling the world makes \(q(z) = \pi^{\sigma}_{-i}(z)\) cancel.

03 /Equilibria, best response, exploitability

A best response to \(\sigma_{-i}\) maximizes player \(i\)'s expected utility, with value \(b_i(\sigma_{-i}) = \max_{\sigma'_i} u_i(\sigma'_i, \sigma_{-i})\). An \(\varepsilon\)-Nash equilibrium is a profile where no player can gain more than \(\varepsilon\) by deviating:

$$\forall i \in N: \quad u_i(\sigma) + \varepsilon \;\ge\; \max_{\sigma'_i \in \Sigma_i} u_i(\sigma'_i, \sigma_{-i}).$$

In two-player zero-sum games, distance from equilibrium is measured by exploitability:

$$\epsilon_{\sigma} \;=\; b_1(\sigma_2) + b_2(\sigma_1),$$

the total amount a pair of best-responding adversaries would win against the profile — zero exactly at Nash. Every convergence plot on this site has exploitability on its y-axis (computed with OpenSpiel's best-response tooling); it is the honest metric because it asks "how badly could this strategy be beaten?", not "how well did it do against one opponent?".
