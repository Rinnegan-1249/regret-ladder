Games

Each game on the ladder is playable against the project's actual trained bots, and each has a live-training view where you can watch its algorithms learn. The games are ordered by the machinery they need: a normal-form game solvable by plain regret matching, then an extensive-form game with hidden information that requires the full CFR family.

Rock–Paper–Scissors plays a real live match here. Kuhn and Leduc Poker step through recorded hands instead of a live match (this static site can't hold hidden game state on a server) — for real interactive play against the bots and live training graphs, try the dynamic version (slower first load — it spins up a live Python server).

✊ Rock–Paper–Scissors

Normal-form · zero-sum · one information set

The canonical mixed-strategy game. Play against a frozen near-Nash bot or an adaptive regret-matching bot that learns from your realized moves (Hart & Mas-Colell 2000) — over-play one hand and watch it punish you. The live-training view streams regret-matching self-play converging to the (1/3, 1/3, 1/3) equilibrium.

Algorithms: regret matching (realized-action and expected-utility updates).

🂠 Kuhn Poker

Extensive-form · imperfect information · 12 information sets

The smallest real poker game — three cards, one betting round, and a Nash equilibrium that requires bluffing. Play against five baseline bots and four CFR-family bots, with your opponent's card revealed only at showdown. The live-training view shows each algorithm's bluffing probabilities locking onto the equilibrium and its exploitability decaying.

Algorithms: vanilla CFR, CFR+, outcome-sampling MCCFR, external-sampling MCCFR.

🃏 Leduc Poker

Extensive-form · two betting rounds · public board card

The next rung: a 6-card deck, a public board card, and two betting rounds — 936 information sets, 78× more than Kuhn. CFR+'s exploitability win over vanilla CFR grows from 255× on Kuhn to 2,164× here.

Algorithms: vanilla CFR, CFR+ (trained bots); full 4-way MCCFR comparison in the training view.
