PRIMARY LITERATURE

References

Primary literature followed by this project. The three papers driving the implementations are attached as PDFs.

Primary papers (PDFs)

  1. [1] Martin Zinkevich, Michael Johanson, Michael Bowling, Carmelo Piccione. Regret Minimization in Games with Incomplete Information. Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems 20 (NIPS), 2007. [PDF]
    Defines counterfactual value and counterfactual regret, proves the regret decomposition theorem, and introduces vanilla CFR. Followed for: the common theory section, regret matching on RPS, and vanilla CFR on Kuhn.
  2. [2] Marc Lanctot, Kevin Waugh, Martin Zinkevich, Michael Bowling. Monte Carlo Sampling for Regret Minimization in Extensive Games. Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems 22 (NIPS), 2009. [PDF]
    Defines the MCCFR family via sampled counterfactual values (unbiasedness Lemma 1), introduces outcome sampling (Eq. 10) and external sampling (Eq. 11) with probabilistic regret bounds (Theorems 4–5). Followed for: both MCCFR solvers.
  3. [3] Oskari Tammelin. Solving Large Imperfect Information Games Using CFR+. arXiv:1407.5042, 2014. [PDF]
    Introduces regret-matching+, alternating updates, and delayed linear averaging; shows order-of-magnitude convergence gains and near-convergence of the current strategy. Followed for: the CFR+ solver.

Supporting literature (cited in the above)

  1. [4] Sergiu Hart, Andreu Mas-Colell. A Simple Adaptive Procedure Leading to Correlated Equilibrium. Econometrica 68(5):1127–1150, 2000.
    The origin of regret matching. Crucially for this site's adaptive RPS bot: the procedure is defined using only the realized history of play and its guarantees hold regardless of what the opponents do — no knowledge of their mixed strategies is required. Reference [2] of Zinkevich et al. and [2]/[7] of Lanctot et al.
  2. [5] Sergiu Hart, Andreu Mas-Colell. A General Class of Adaptive Strategies. Journal of Economic Theory 98:26–54, 2001.
    Generalizes regret matching to a class of no-external-regret strategies via Blackwell approachability.
  3. [6] David Blackwell. An Analog of the Minimax Theorem for Vector Payoffs. Pacific Journal of Mathematics 6:1–8, 1956.
    Approachability theory — the foundation for why regret matching's average regret vanishes against arbitrary opponent sequences ("Regret-matching satisfies Blackwell's approachability criteria", Lanctot et al., footnote 2).
  4. [7] Harold W. Kuhn. Simplified Two-Person Poker. Contributions to the Theory of Games 1:97–103, 1950.
    The game itself, including the one-parameter family of Nash equilibria recovered by our solvers.
