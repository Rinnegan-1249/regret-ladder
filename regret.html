Regret & Counterfactual Regret

The literature ladder this project climbs: from no-regret learning (Blackwell 1956; Hart & Mas-Colell 2000) to counterfactual regret minimization (Zinkevich et al. 2007) and its accelerated and sampled descendants (Tammelin 2014; Lanctot et al. 2009).

01 /Regret: doing as well as hindsight

Play a game repeatedly with strategy \(\sigma^t_i\) at round \(t\). Player \(i\)'s average overall (external) regret after \(T\) rounds compares what they got to the best single fixed strategy in hindsight:

$$R^T_i \;=\; \frac{1}{T}\,\max_{\sigma^*_i \in \Sigma_i} \sum_{t=1}^{T}\Big(u_i(\sigma^*_i, \sigma^t_{-i}) - u_i(\sigma^t)\Big).$$

An algorithm is regret minimizing if \(R^T_i \to 0\) regardless of the opponents' play. The connection to equilibria is the folk theorem that powers everything here:

Theorem (regret → Nash; [1] Thm. 1). In a two-player zero-sum game, if both players' average regret is at most \(\varepsilon\), the average strategy profile \(\bar{\sigma}^T\) is a \(2\varepsilon\)-Nash equilibrium, where each information set's average is weighted by the player's own reach: \(\bar{\sigma}^t_i(a|I) = \sum_t \pi^{\sigma^t}_i(I)\sigma^t(a|I) \,/\, \sum_t \pi^{\sigma^t}_i(I)\). Hence self-play between two regret minimizers computes an equilibrium — and it is always the average strategy, not the current one, that converges (CFR+ is the partial empirical exception).

02 /Regret matching (Hart & Mas-Colell 2000)

The simplest regret minimizer, and the engine inside every CFR variant. Keep a cumulative regret \(R^t(a)\) per action; play proportionally to the positive part:

$$\sigma^{t+1}(a) \;=\; \frac{R^{t,+}(a)}{\sum_{a'} R^{t,+}(a')}, \qquad x^+ = \max(x, 0),$$

(uniform when no positive regret). Its guarantee rests on Blackwell's approachability theorem (1956): the average regret vector approaches the non-positive orthant almost surely, giving \(R^T \le \Delta_u \sqrt{|A|}/\sqrt{T}\) against arbitrary opponents. Hart & Mas-Colell (2001) generalize this to a whole class of no-regret strategies.

Observability. Crucially, the procedure is defined from realized play only: after the opponent's action \(b_t\) is revealed, the full counterfactual payoff vector \(u(\cdot, b_t)\) is computable — no knowledge of the opponent's mixed strategy is needed. That is what licenses the adaptive RPS bot to learn against you; self-play with expected-utility updates (used in our Week 2 experiment for smoother convergence) is the special case where the opponent's distribution is known.

03 /Counterfactual regret (Zinkevich et al. 2007)

Extensive games have exponentially many pure strategies, so minimizing overall regret directly is hopeless. CFR's insight: define a local regret at each information set that (a) can be minimized by plain regret matching, and (b) bounds the overall regret. The counterfactual value of information set \(I\) weights outcomes by the probability that everyone else (opponent and chance) reaches it:

$$v_i(\sigma, I) \;=\; \sum_{z \in Z_I} \pi^{\sigma}_{-i}(z[I])\;\pi^{\sigma}(z[I], z)\;u_i(z),$$

— "counterfactual" because it asks what \(I\) is worth were the player to play to reach it. The immediate counterfactual regret of action \(a\) at \(I\) compares always-playing- \(a\) to the current strategy:

$$R^T_{i,\mathrm{imm}}(I,a) \;=\; \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \Big( v_i(\sigma^t_{(I \to a)}, I) - v_i(\sigma^t, I) \Big).$$
Theorem (decomposition; [1] Thm. 3). \(R^T_i \;\le\; \sum_{I \in \mathcal{I}_i} R^{T,+}_{i,\mathrm{imm}}(I).\) Overall regret is bounded by the sum of per-information-set counterfactual regrets — so running independent regret matching at every information set minimizes overall regret, with bound \(R^T_i \le \Delta_{u,i}\,|\mathcal{I}_i|\sqrt{|A_i|}/\sqrt{T}\) (tightened to \(\Delta_{u,i}\,M_i\sqrt{|A_i|}/\sqrt{T}\) by Lanctot et al., Thm. 3). Combined with the folk theorem, self-play CFR converges to equilibrium at rate \(O(1/\sqrt{T})\). This is vanilla CFR: full tree traversal per iteration, regrets accumulated at every information set.

04 /Descendants: CFR+ and sampled counterfactual values

CFR+ (Tammelin 2014 [3]) replaces regret matching with regret-matching+ — cumulative regrets floored at zero each update, \(R^{+,T}(I,a) = \max\{R^{+,T-1}(I,a) + v_i(\sigma^T_{I\to a}, I) - v_i(\sigma^T, I),\, 0\}\) — with alternating per-player updates and linearly weighted averaging \(w_t = \max(t-d, 0)\). Empirically an order of magnitude faster, and its current strategy (nearly) converges directly.

MCCFR (Lanctot et al. 2009 [2]) replaces exact counterfactual values with sampled ones: partition terminal histories into blocks \(\mathcal{Q}\), sample a block with probability \(q_j\), and reweight by \(1/q(z)\). The estimator is unbiased (\(\mathbb{E}[\tilde{v}_i] = v_i\), their Lemma 1), so regret updates match CFR in expectation while touching a fraction of the tree: outcome sampling walks a single history per iteration (\(\varepsilon\)-greedy, \(1/\delta\) factor in its bound, Thm. 5); external sampling samples only chance and opponent so the weights cancel exactly, needing just a constant factor more iterations at \(O(\sqrt{|H|})\) per-iteration cost (Thm. 4).

The full per-algorithm treatments live with their game: regret matching → RPS, CFR / CFR+ / MCCFR → Kuhn Poker; measured outcomes are on the Results page.
