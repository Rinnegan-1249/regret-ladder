Results

Every figure below was produced by this project's seeded experiment scripts — nothing is illustrative or mocked. Numbers cite the iteration budgets used.

Rock–Paper–Scissors: regret matching (Week 2)

Expected-utility regret-matching self-play, 100,000 iterations, 5 seeds. Findings:

  • Average strategy → Nash. All seeds converge to the unique equilibrium \((1/3, 1/3, 1/3)\) to within \(10^{-3}\) by \(10^5\) iterations, from very different seeded starting perturbations.
  • Average regret decays like \(1/T\) with expected updates (the sampled variant's guarantee is \(O(1/\sqrt{T})\)) — the log–log decay line is straight over four decades.

Kuhn Poker: vanilla CFR (Week 3, Zinkevich et al. [1])

From-scratch tabular CFR, validated against OpenSpiel's CFRSolver: the two exploitability traces coincide across all checkpoints. Exploitability of the average strategy falls as \(O(1/\sqrt{T})\)–to–\(O(1/T)\), reaching 2.3×10⁻³ chips/hand at 10,000 iterations.

Game value (P1)—−0.055556 = −1/18 exactly

The recovered CFR+ average strategy is an exact member of Kuhn's Nash family: \(\alpha = P(\text{bet}|J) = 0.2226\), \(P(\text{bet}|Q) = 0\), \(P(\text{bet}|K) = 0.6678 = 3\alpha\). The paper's two empirical claims both reproduce: the average strategy converges an order of magnitude (here ~2.5 orders) faster, and the current strategy (nearly) converges — vanilla CFR's current strategy never does. The compressibility claim (§3 of the paper) also reproduces: a large fraction of CFR+'s regret entries stay exactly zero while CFR accumulates negative regret indefinitely.

Kuhn Poker: MCCFR (Week 4, Lanctot et al. [2])

Four-way comparison at each algorithm's budget (CFR/CFR+ 10k iterations; OS-MCCFR 200k ×3 seeds; ES-MCCFR 50k ×3 seeds), on the paper's own metric — exploitability vs nodes touched:

AlgorithmIterationsFinal avg-strategy exploitabilityNodes touched
Vanilla CFR10,0002.3×10⁻³5.8×10⁵
CFR+10,0009.1×10⁻⁶1.2×10⁶
OS-MCCFR200,0005.9×10⁻³ (3-seed mean)2.1×10⁶
ES-MCCFR50,0002.5×10⁻³ (3-seed mean)7.3×10⁵
The honest finding: on Kuhn, MCCFR does not beat full-traversal CFR per node touched — ES roughly ties vanilla CFR and OS trails with sampling noise, while CFR+ dominates by ~2.5 orders of magnitude. This is what the theory predicts: Kuhn's tree has 58 nodes, so a "full traversal" costs almost nothing and sampling can only save a factor of ~4–5, less than the variance penalty it introduces (plus OS's \(1/\delta\) bound inflation, Theorem 5). The paper's dramatic wins use games with \(10^7\)–\(10^8\) histories (their "One-Card Poker" is Kuhn with a 500-card deck). Even the paper itself shows OS losing to pruned CFR on Latent Tic-Tac-Toe. Verdict: MCCFR's advantage is an asymptotic-in-game-size claim — the motivation for scaling to Leduc Poker next.

Both MCCFR implementations were validated against OpenSpiel's OutcomeSamplingSolver and ExternalSamplingSolver at matching iteration counts (same order of magnitude at every checkpoint). Our CFR+ also tracks OpenSpiel's CFRPlusSolver: at 10/100/1,000/10,000 iterations ours reads 2.9×10⁻²/9.6×10⁻⁴/7.1×10⁻⁵/9.1×10⁻⁶ vs OpenSpiel's 3.3×10⁻²/1.2×10⁻³/8.7×10⁻⁵/9.6×10⁻⁶.

Leduc Poker: scaling CFR/CFR+ and MCCFR (Weeks 5–6)

Leduc Poker has 9,457 game-tree nodes and 936 information sets — 394× and 78× larger than Kuhn, respectively — and all four solvers run on it with zero code changes (they were already game-agnostic). CFR+'s exploitability win over vanilla CFR grows with game size: 255× on Kuhn → 2,164× on Leduc at 10,000 iterations.

Metric @ 10,000 iterationsVanilla CFRCFR+
Average-strategy exploitability1.04×10⁻²4.82×10⁻⁶ (≈2,164× better)
Current-strategy exploitability1.10×10⁰ (oscillates)2.06×10⁻³ (decaying)

The full 4-way comparison (CFR, CFR+, OS-MCCFR, ES-MCCFR) by nodes touched mirrors the honest finding from Kuhn, now at a larger scale: full-traversal solvers remain more node-efficient than MCCFR on a game this size, though the gap between sampling and full traversal narrows as the tree grows.

Both CFR and CFR+ (5,000 iterations each) dominate all five baseline agents in a 7-agent round-robin tournament, by almost identical margins, and are statistically indistinguishable head-to-head (mean −0.00058 chips/hand, not significant) — both have already converged to an effectively Nash-equivalent strategy at this budget.
