Regret matching (Hart & Mas-Colell 2000; Zinkevich et al. 2007 §2)

RPS is a normal-form game — an extensive game with a single information set per player — so CFR reduces exactly to regret matching. Maintain a cumulative regret per action; play each action with probability proportional to its positive cumulative regret:

$$\sigma^{t}(a) \;=\; \frac{R^{t-1,+}(a)}{\sum_{a'} R^{t-1,+}(a')} \quad \text{(uniform if all positive regrets are zero)},$$

then update regrets. Two update rules appear in this project:

  • Realized-action (sampled) updates — after observing the opponent's realized action \(b_t\): \(R^t(a) \mathrel{+}= u(a, b_t) - u(a_t, b_t)\). This is the Hart & Mas-Colell setting and what the adaptive bot in the Play tab does.
  • Expected-utility updates — against the opponent's full mixed strategy \(\sigma_{-i}^t\): \(R^t(a) \mathrel{+}= u(a, \sigma_{-i}^t) - u(\sigma_i^t, \sigma_{-i}^t)\). Only possible when the distribution is known — i.e., in self-play, which is what the live training tab runs (deterministic, smoother convergence).
Observability: realized actions vs. strategies. A natural objection: Zinkevich et al. define regret minimization as average regret vanishing "regardless of the sequence \(\sigma^t_{-i}\)" — but in real play you never observe the opponent's mixed strategy, only their realized actions. The resolution is that regret matching does not need \(\sigma_{-i}\): it needs the counterfactual payoff vector \(u(a, b_t)\) for all of your own actions \(a\), which is fully computable in RPS the moment the opponent's realized action \(b_t\) is revealed. Hart & Mas-Colell (2000) define the procedure exactly this way and prove its guarantees for arbitrary (even adaptive) opponents; the underlying result is Blackwell's (1956) approachability theorem — see Lanctot et al., footnote 2, and Hart & Mas-Colell (2001) for the general no-external-regret class. The guarantee: $$\max_a \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\big(u(a, b_t) - u(a_t, b_t)\big) \;\to\; 0 \quad \text{a.s.},$$ i.e. the adaptive bot asymptotically does at least as well as the best fixed action in hindsight against your actual play. If you over-play Rock, the best fixed action in hindsight is Paper — so the bot's strategy drifts there. Self-play with expected updates is the special case where the opponent's distribution happens to be known.

Why the average strategy converges to Nash. In zero-sum self-play, both players' average regrets vanish at rate \(O(1/\sqrt{T})\) (with expected updates, in practice \(O(1/T)\) here), so by the folk theorem in the Regret section the average strategy profile converges to the unique Nash equilibrium \((1/3, 1/3, 1/3)\). Note it is the average strategy — current strategies cycle.

Results (Week 2 experiments)

Five seeded runs of 100,000 expected-update iterations: average strategies converge to within \(10^{-3}\) of uniform; average regret decays like \(1/T\) on the log–log plot. Full figures and discussion on the Results page.
