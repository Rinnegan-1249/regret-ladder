Regret Ladder

An interactive companion to a summer research project on regret minimization and counterfactual regret minimization (CFR) in games.

What this site is

This project builds a ladder of increasingly capable game-solving algorithms — starting from regret matching on a normal-form game and climbing to the CFR family on an imperfect-information poker game — with every step implemented from scratch, validated against OpenSpiel's reference solvers, and accompanied by reproducible experiments. This site lets you play against the actual trained bots, watch the algorithms train live, and read the theory behind each rung, following the primary literature.

Rung 1Normal-form games, Nash equilibria, best responses
Rung 2Regret matching on Rock–Paper–Scissors (Hart & Mas-Colell 2000)
Rung 3Vanilla CFR on Kuhn Poker (Zinkevich et al. 2007)
Rung 4CFR+ (Tammelin 2014) and Monte Carlo CFR (Lanctot et al. 2009)
NextLeduc Poker, abstraction, simplified Hold'em

Games

Rock–Paper–Scissors

Play against a frozen near-Nash bot or an adaptive regret-matching bot that learns from your realized moves. Watch regret matching converge to the (1/3, 1/3, 1/3) equilibrium live.

Kuhn Poker

Three cards, one betting round, real bluffing. Play against five baseline bots and four CFR-family bots (vanilla CFR, CFR+, OS-MCCFR, ES-MCCFR), and watch each algorithm train live.

Common theory: regret and equilibria

Everything on this site rests on one framework, due to Zinkevich, Johanson, Bowling & Piccione (2007) [1], building on Hart & Mas-Colell (2000) [4]. The notation below follows [1].

Extensive games and strategies

An extensive game with imperfect information consists of histories \(h\) of actions, terminal histories \(z \in Z\) with utilities \(u_i(z)\), a player function (including chance \(c\)), and information sets \(I \in \mathcal{I}_i\) — sets of histories player \(i\) cannot tell apart, at which the player must therefore act with a single distribution. A strategy \(\sigma_i\) assigns a distribution over actions to each information set; \(\pi^{\sigma}(h)\) is the reach probability of \(h\), factored as \(\pi^{\sigma}_i(h)\,\pi^{\sigma}_{-i}(h)\) into the player's own contribution and everyone else's (opponent and chance).

Average overall regret

If profile \(\sigma^t\) is played at round \(t\), player \(i\)'s average overall regret after \(T\) rounds is

$$R^T_i \;=\; \frac{1}{T}\,\max_{\sigma^*_i \in \Sigma_i} \sum_{t=1}^{T}\Big(u_i(\sigma^*_i, \sigma^t_{-i}) - u_i(\sigma^t)\Big),$$

and the average strategy \(\bar{\sigma}^T_i\) weights each round's strategy by the player's own reach:

$$\bar{\sigma}^t_i(a\,|\,I) \;=\; \frac{\sum_{t=1}^{T} \pi^{\sigma^t}_i(I)\,\sigma^t(a\,|\,I)}{\sum_{t=1}^{T} \pi^{\sigma^t}_i(I)}.$$
Theorem 1 (folk theorem; [1] Thm. 1). In a two-player zero-sum game, if both players' average overall regret is at most \(\varepsilon\), then the average strategy profile \(\bar{\sigma}^T\) is a \(2\varepsilon\)-Nash equilibrium. This is why every algorithm on this site outputs its average strategy (CFR+ being a partial exception — its current strategy also converges in practice).

Counterfactual regret

CFR's key construction: the counterfactual value of information set \(I\) is the expected utility given that player \(i\) plays to reach \(I\), weighted by the opponent's and chance's probability of reaching it ([1] Eq. 4):

$$v_i(\sigma, I) \;=\; \sum_{z \in Z_I} \pi^{\sigma}_{-i}(z[I])\;\pi^{\sigma}(z[I], z)\;u_i(z),$$

and the immediate counterfactual regret of action \(a\) at \(I\) is ([1] Eq. 5)

$$R^T_{i,\mathrm{imm}}(I,a) \;=\; \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \Big( v_i(\sigma^t_{(I \to a)}, I) - v_i(\sigma^t, I) \Big).$$
Theorem 2 (regret decomposition; [1] Thm. 3). \(R^T_i \le \sum_{I \in \mathcal{I}_i} R^{T,+}_{i,\mathrm{imm}}(I)\) where \(x^+ = \max(x, 0)\). Overall regret is bounded by the sum of per-information-set regrets — so independently minimizing regret at each information set (with regret matching) minimizes overall regret. With regret matching at each information set, \(R^T_i \le \Delta_{u,i}\,|\mathcal{I}_i|\sqrt{|A_i|}/\sqrt{T}\), giving \(O(1/\sqrt{T})\) convergence to equilibrium.

The per-game pages develop the specific algorithms: regret matching (the single-information-set special case, on RPS) and vanilla CFR, CFR+, and MCCFR (on Kuhn Poker), each following its primary paper.
