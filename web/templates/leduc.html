Leduc Poker

A 6-card deck, a public board card, and two betting rounds — 936 information sets, 78× more than Kuhn. Watch recorded hands from the trained bots, and the same CFR family training at this larger scale.

Rules

Leduc Hold'em (Southey et al. 2005) uses a 6-card deck: two suits of Jack, Queen, King (suit is irrelevant to hand strength). Both players ante 1 chip and are dealt one private card. There are two betting rounds: the first on the private cards alone, the second after a single public board card is revealed. A pair (private card matching the board) beats any unpaired hand; unpaired hands are ranked by card alone. Bets are a fixed size per round (2 chips in round 1, 4 in round 2), with a cap of 2 raises per round.

Leduc's tree has 9,457 nodes and 936 information sets — 394× and 78× larger than Kuhn — large enough that the asymptotic differences between full-traversal CFR/CFR+ and the sampling-based MCCFR variants start to show up, though (as the training tab shows) not yet enough to flip the verdict from Kuhn: full traversal still wins per node touched at this scale.

All four solvers at Leduc's scale

Leduc has too many information sets (936) to chart per-infoset bet probabilities the way the Kuhn page does, so this replays the project's actual validated training runs instead — CFR, CFR+, OS-MCCFR, and ES-MCCFR — pulled directly from results/tables/week05_leduc_cfr_variants.csv (CFR/CFR+, 10,000 iterations) and results/tables/week06_leduc_mccfr.csv (OS-MCCFR/ES-MCCFR, 500,000/100,000 iterations, seed 0) — animated checkpoint by checkpoint. CFR and CFR+ finish well before the MCCFR variants do (their curves hold steady once their own run ends) since the three runs use very different iteration budgets.

CFR+'s exploitability win over vanilla CFR grows with game size: 255× on Kuhn → 2,164× on Leduc at 10,000 iterations — the regret floor matters more as more information sets accumulate negative regret that vanilla CFR would otherwise keep oscillating around. The honest MCCFR finding from Kuhn holds here too: despite far larger iteration budgets, OS-MCCFR and ES-MCCFR don't catch full-traversal CFR+'s exploitability — full traversal remains more efficient per node touched at this scale. See Results for the same comparison by nodes touched instead of iterations.

Same algorithms, larger game

Every solver here — VanillaCFR, CFRPlus, OutcomeSamplingMCCFR, ExternalSamplingMCCFR — is the exact same game-agnostic implementation used on Kuhn (see the Kuhn page and Regret section for the algorithm definitions); pointing them at leduc_poker instead of kuhn_poker required zero solver code changes — they operate purely through OpenSpiel's generic information-state interface.

What changes with scale: vanilla CFR's average-strategy exploitability is the same order of magnitude as OpenSpiel's reference solver throughout training, but the gap between full-traversal solvers and MCCFR's sampling variants narrows — MCCFR needs much larger iteration budgets here (500,000 for OS-MCCFR, 100,000 for ES-MCCFR, vs 10,000 for CFR/CFR+) to reach comparable exploitability, since sample variance grows with the size of the tree being sampled. See Results for the full numbers.
